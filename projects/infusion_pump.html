Positive-Displacement Infusion Pump (Veterinary)

Role: System design & analysis

Peristaltic pump

Prototype rotary peristaltic infusion pump for veterinary applications, showing the rotor, and housing layout and gearbox.

Overview

Compact, accurate fluid delivery for veterinary use

A compact infusion/dispense device for veterinary use. The pump delivers precise small-volume doses and supports two operating modes: Direct Infusion mode (rate-controlled) and Dispense mode (volume-controlled). The design emphasizes accuracy at low flow, high back-pressure tolerance, low power, and leakage resistance.

Goals

Objectives

  • Develop a positive-displacement proof of concept modular pump and drivetrain that ensures volumetric accuracy at small doses and quick fill time.
  • Ensure accurate, repeatable flow in sterile operation with quick fill time.
  • Prioritize low manufacturing cost, ease of use, and long service life.
  • Minimize electrical power draw via friction reduction and lightweight drivetrain components.
  • Design for robustness, easy assembly, and high back-pressure resistance before leakage.
Summary

Key Works Completed

    Groundwork & System Definition:

  • Identified critical design parameters such as flow rate, accuracy, sterility, and operating life. Selected a peristaltic pump as the optimal technology for its precision, simplicity, and hygiene.

    • Mechanical Design:

  • Developed a complete CAD model featuring a adjustable roller count rotor, nylon shaft, 3D-printed couplers and housing, and laser-cut acrylic plates.
  • Designed for modular assembly and easy maintenance, with a quick-release tube housing and replaceable tubing.
  • Integrated a custom gearbox and performed power-torque analysis to meet flow requirements up to 50 mL/min.

    • Manufacturing & Environmental Considerations:

  • Used 3D printing and laser cutting for rapid prototyping.

    • Engineering Analysis:

  • Created Excel-based macros to model power losses from rolling friction and tube compression.
  • Conducted a first-principles power analysis (hydraulic, compressive, and frictional) and verified results against literature.
  • Performed shaft stress analysis to ensure minimal torsional deflection under maximum load.
  • Constructed a physical prototype and ran tests to obtain a pump performance curve.
Analysis

Engineering Analysis

A full first-principles engineering analysis was conducted to determine the power, torque, and structural requirements of the pump. The analysis combined fluid mechanics, viscoelastic material modelling, drivetrain mechanics, and iterative spreadsheet simulations.

    Problem Identification:

  • Defined the total power requirement as the sum of hydraulic power, tube compression power, and rolling-friction losses.
  • Identified modelling challenges due to the viscoelastic behaviour of silicone tubing, which cannot be described using Hooke’s Law.

    • Hydraulic Power:

  • Applied steady-state energy and pressure-loss equations to model head loss from laminar flow.
  • Derived machine-head requirements for URS-specified lift heights (1–2 m).

    • Rolling Friction Losses:

  • Used the Brush Model to calculate energy losses from roller-tube damping effects
  • Computed dissipative factors using the Fractional-Order Differential Zener (FDZ) model.
  • Validated analytical values by comparing them against published experimental silicone viscoelastic data.

    • Compression Power:

  • Built a Newtonian force-balance model to approximate the deformation work done by each roller.
  • Integrated the normal-force distribution over the contact patch to compute compressive work at different rotor speeds.

    • Shaft Stress Analysis:

  • Analysed torsional stress and angle of twist for the nylon shaft under maximum torque.
  • Verified that the shaft remains below yield and exhibits negligible twist during operation.

    • Rotor Speed & Flow Calculations:

  • Derived relationships between flow rate, roller spacing, rotor speed, and trapped volume.
  • Generated an Excel calculation sheet to compute power and torque across the full operating range.

    • Key Outcomes:

  • Confirmed the design meets all URS performance limits within available gearbox output power.
  • Identified roller count as a critical parameter—higher accuracy but significantly higher power draw.
  • Validated analytical predictions through trend-matching with experimental viscoelastic data.
Drivetrain

Gearbox Sizing & Drivetrain

The drivetrain was designed to deliver sufficient torque and speed to operate the peristaltic pump across its full flow-rate range while remaining compact and energy-efficient.

    Engineering Insight:

  • The gearbox provides a strong balance between torque output and compactness.
  • Motor and gearbox selection were constrained by the URS budget, driving a lightweight, low-cost, but sufficiently powerful drivetrain.
Mechanism

Rotor & Pumping Mechanism

The pump uses a multi-roller rotor to provide high-resolution fluid dosing. The rotor layout ensures consistent tube occlusion and smooth fluid displacement.

    Rotor Construction:

  • Three-plate acrylic rotor architecture cut using laser-cutting for precision.
  • Multiple compression rollers mounted on mild-steel axles to reduce slip and avoid custom-machined rollers.
  • Square shaft profile ensures positive engagement and avoids backlash.

    • Performance Considerations:

  • More rollers → higher accuracy but increased power consumption.
  • Ten rollers initially chosen as optimal trade-off between precision and efficiency before testing but this can be changed after tests without any additinal manufacturing.

    • Tube Loading & Occlusion:

  • Removable tube housing with a slider mechanism for quick, sterile tubing replacement.
  • Tube geometry ensures full occlusion without damaging the pipe or requiring seals.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Laser-cut plates provide sharp internal corners, enabling a square shaft fit that is difficult to machine using traditional methods.
  • Acrylic plates assist low friction and low inertia, improving energy efficiency.
Power

Power Modelling & Viscoelastic Behaviour

Power analysis combined fluid mechanics, viscoelastic material modelling, and rolling-contact mechanics to predict the pump’s operating requirements.

    Hydraulic Power:

  • Modelled pressure loss from laminar flow using Reynolds-number verification.
  • Included minor losses from the 180° tube bend and outlet discharge.
  • Calculated machine-head power needed to lift fluid up to 2 m.

    • Rolling Friction:

  • Applied the Brush Model to estimate hysteresis losses in silicone tubing.
  • Dissipation factor computed using the Fractional-Order Differential Zener (FDZ) viscoelastic model.
  • Analytical tan(δ) validated against experimental silicone rubber data.

    • Compression Power:

  • Constructed a force-balance model using roller–tube contact patch geometry.
  • Integrated normal force distribution to determine work done per revolution.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Tube viscoelasticity dominates total power consumption—accurate modelling is essential.
  • Experimental trends aligned well with analytical predictions, increasing confidence in the model.
  • Power rises exponentially with both flow rate and roller count.
Materials

Material Selection & Component Durability

Materials were chosen to maximise service life, sterility, low noise, and manufacturability, while staying within the project’s budget constraints.

    Nylon Shaft:

  • Selected for low friction, low noise, and self-lubricating properties.
  • Stress analysis confirmed negligible twist under max load.
  • Lightweight, reducing bending and vibration risk.

    • Acrylic Rotor Plates:

  • Laser-cut for geometric precision and fast prototyping.
  • Sharp internal corners enable square shaft engagement.

    • Silicone Tubing:

  • Ensures sterility—only the tube contacts medication.
  • Compatible with a wide range of veterinary fluids.
  • Easy to replace through the quick-release tube housing.

    • PLA Housing Components:

  • 3D-printed for rapid design iteration and low cost.
  • Supports easy assembly and prototype modification.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Viscoelastic materials require empirical cross-checking—analytical models alone are insufficient.
  • Material choices minimise wear and noise, improving usability in clinical environments.
  • Manufacturing strategy allows smooth transition from prototype to injection-moulded mass production.
