Engineering Project

Wear Tester Rehabilitation & Arduino Control System

Role: Mechanical restoration · Embedded control integration · Vibration analysis

Experimental rigs Embedded systems Vibration

Linear reciprocating wear tester after refurbishment and integration of an Arduino-based control and data acquisition system.

Overview

From unusable rig to instrumented test platform

The function of a wear tester is to evaluate the wear resistance and frictional characteristics of materials under controlled loading conditions. Relative sliding motion between two contact surfaces is simulated under specified load, speed and environmental conditions.

The wear testers designated to be refurbished exhibited faulty machine elements, excessive vibration, and an outdated electronic control system. As a result, they were either non-operational or could not be relied upon for accurate results. Limited compatibility between the legacy electronics and the Arduino controller with SD card reader further restricted test traceability and data integrity.

This project involved the mechanical repair of the wear tester and the development and integration of an Arduino Uno microcontroller system for real-time control, sensing, and data acquisition. The embedded platform interfaces with a Hall-effect sensor, relay module, keypad, SD card module, and LCD display to support automated testing and data recording in a mechanical system.

Goals

Objectives

  • Restore and recommission a non-operational wear tester.
  • Identify and replace corroded or failed mechanical parts.
  • Integrate an Arduino-based embedded control and sensing platform.
  • Conduct vibration analysis to evaluate the benefits of adding a vibration absorber.
  • Achieve a fully functional system capable of automated data logging and feedback control.

Mechanical

Mechanical Rehabilitation & Rebuild

The project began with a full teardown and mechanical restoration of a non-operational wear tester. The goal was to return the rig to a mechanically sound, test-ready state before any electronics or automation were added.

    Disassembly & Inspection:

  • Completely disassembled the wear tester to component level and documented the assembly layout.
  • Identified which parts were structurally sound and which custom components had failed or deformed.

    • Corrosion Removal & Surface Restoration:

  • Removed surface corrosion using sanding and grinding techniques on key structural and rotating components.
  • Restored contact surfaces and bearing seats to maintain alignment and fit.

    • Protection & Replacement:

  • Applied chemical pickling and protective coatings to exposed steel surfaces to prevent further corrosion.
  • Procured and installed new parts to replace failed or non-repairable custom components.

    • Reassembly & Alignment:

  • Reassembled the tester, restoring correct load paths and mechanical clearances.
  • Performed alignment checks to ensure shafts, bearings, and fixtures were correctly located before commissioning.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Systematic strip-down and refurbishment turned a non-functional rig into a reliable test platform without full replacement.
  • Corrosion control and surface preparation directly improved repeatability and reduced future maintenance load.
Dynamics

Vibration Analysis & Absorber Evaluation

After mechanical restoration, vibration behaviour was analysed to assess the benefits of adding a vibration absorber to improve measurement quality and reduce structural fatigue.

    Baseline Vibration Characterisation:

  • Ran the tester at representative operating speeds to measure baseline vibration levels.
  • Identified dominant vibration frequencies associated with the drive system and test fixture.

    • Vibration Absorber Integration:

  • Incorporated a vibration absorber tuned near the dominant excitation frequency.
  • Mounted and constrained the absorber to maintain consistent dynamic behaviour during tests.

    • Comparative Testing:

  • Recorded vibration response with and without the absorber installed.
  • Assessed amplitude reduction and changes in frequency content to quantify absorber performance.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Properly tuned damping reduced vibration amplitude, helping protect sensitive components and improving data quality.
  • Demonstrated how small passive additions can significantly enhance the dynamic behaviour of older test equipment.
Embedded systems

Arduino-Based Control & Data Acquisition

A modern Arduino-based embedded system was integrated into the wear tester to enable real-time sensing, user input, and data logging without relying on legacy electronics.

    Hardware Integration:

  • Configured an Arduino Uno as the central controller for sensing and actuation.
  • Interfaced a Hall-effect sensor (D2) for rotational speed measurement.
  • Used a relay module (A0) to switch external loads and control test cycles.
  • Connected a keypad (pins 3–9) for user parameter input and mode selection.
  • Integrated an SD card module (pins 10–13) for autonomous data logging.
  • Added an LCD display to provide live status, prompts, and measurement feedback.

    • Firmware Development:

  • Developed Arduino C++ code using interrupt-based measurement for accurate RPM capture.
  • Implemented control logic for relay actuation based on user-defined thresholds and test routines.
  • Structured data logging to write results to SD in .csv format for later analysis.
  • Used serial communication for debugging, calibration, and sanity checks during commissioning.

    • Engineering Insight:

  • Upgrading legacy hardware with low-cost microcontroller electronics extended the useful life of the tester.
  • Modular firmware and pin layout allow additional sensors or control channels to be added with minimal redesign.
